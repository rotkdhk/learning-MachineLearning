## The Shape of the Cost Function

Let's use a tiny, clean dataset to actually **see** the cost function's shape — not just imagine it.

$$x = [1, 2, 3] \qquad y = [2, 4, 6]$$

(Notice: $y$ is exactly $2x$, so we already know the "perfect" answer is $w=2, b=0$ — but let's pretend we don't know that yet, and let the cost function reveal it.)

Fixing $b = 0$:

$$J(w) = \frac{1}{2m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}(w\cdot x_i - y_i)^2$$

| $w$ | Predictions ($w \cdot x$) | Actual ($y$) | Errors (pred − actual) | $J(w)$ |
|-----|----------------------------|--------------|--------------------------|--------|
| 0.0 | 0, 0, 0     | 2, 4, 6 | −2, −4, −6 | 9.33 |
| 1.0 | 1, 2, 3     | 2, 4, 6 | −1, −2, −3 | 2.33 |
| 1.5 | 1.5, 3, 4.5 | 2, 4, 6 | −0.5, −1, −1.5 | 0.58 |
| **2.0** | **2, 4, 6** | **2, 4, 6** | **0, 0, 0** | **0.00** |
| 2.5 | 2.5, 5, 7.5 | 2, 4, 6 | 0.5, 1, 1.5 | 0.58 |
| 3.0 | 3, 6, 9     | 2, 4, 6 | 1, 2, 3 | 2.33 |
| 4.0 | 4, 8, 12    | 2, 4, 6 | 2, 4, 6 | 9.33 |

Now the table actually tells the full story: at $w=2$, every single **prediction matches the actual value exactly** — that's *why* $J(2)=0$. Move away in either direction, and predictions and actuals drift apart — that's *why* $J(w)$ grows.

### Why is it a perfect parabola? (The algebra)

This isn't a coincidence — we can prove it. Since $y = 2x$ exactly for our dataset, every error term simplifies:

$$\text{error}_i = w x_i - y_i = w x_i - 2x_i = (w-2)x_i$$

So the error at *any* point is just $(w-2)$ times that point's $x$-value. Substitute this into $J(w)$:

$$J(w) = \frac{1}{2m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\left[(w-2)x_i\right]^2 = \frac{1}{2m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}(w-2)^2 x_i^2$$

$(w-2)^2$ doesn't depend on $i$, so it pulls outside the sum:

$$J(w) = \frac{(w-2)^2}{2m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}x_i^2$$

Now plug in our actual numbers: $m=3$, and $\sum x_i^2 = 1^2+2^2+3^2 = 1+4+9=14$:

$$J(w) = \frac{(w-2)^2}{6}\times 14 = 2.333\,(w-2)^2$$

**This is exactly the parabola shape** — squaring $(w-2)$ is precisely what produces the symmetric bowl: it's zero at $w=2$, and grows equally whether $w$ is above or below 2, because squaring erases the sign of $(w-2)$ the same way it erased the sign of each individual error earlier (recall: least squares).

Verify against the table: at $w=0$: $2.333\times(0-2)^2 = 2.333\times4 = 9.33$ ✓. At $w=4$: $2.333\times(4-2)^2=2.333\times4=9.33$ ✓ — same value, confirming the symmetry.

### The flat area near the bottom

Zoom into values very close to $w=2$, using the same formula:

| $w$ | $(w-2)^2$ | $J(w)$ |
|-----|-----------|--------|
| 1.90 | 0.01 | 0.0233 |
| 1.95 | 0.0025 | 0.0058 |
| **2.00** | **0** | **0.0000** |
| 2.05 | 0.0025 | 0.0058 |
| 2.10 | 0.01 | 0.0233 |

Out at $w=0$ vs $w=1$, moving $w$ by a full unit changed $J$ by $7$. Here, moving $w$ by just $0.05$ barely changes $J$ at all. This is $(w-2)^2$ itself — squaring a small number makes it *much* smaller ($0.05^2 = 0.0025$), while squaring a large number makes it *much* larger ($2^2=4$). That's the mathematical reason the bowl is steep far away and flat near the bottom — it falls directly out of the squaring in the formula, not just an observation about the numbers.

This is exactly why gradient descent naturally slows down near the minimum: the derivative of $J(w) = 2.333(w-2)^2$ is $J'(w) = 4.666(w-2)$ — and as $w \to 2$, this derivative shrinks toward zero on its own, so each update step gets smaller automatically, even with a constant learning rate $\alpha$.

### Putting it together

- **Bowl shape** → one smooth minimum, no false bottoms to get stuck in — proven algebraically above, not just observed in a table
- **Steep sides** → far from the answer, $(w-2)^2$ is large, so the derivative is large and gradient descent takes big corrective steps
- **Flat bottom** → near the answer, $(w-2)^2$ shrinks fast, so the derivative shrinks too — this is what "convergence" looks like
